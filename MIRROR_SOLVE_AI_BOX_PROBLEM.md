## The Problem (Empirical Status: UNSOLVED since 2002)

The AI-in-a-box experiment (Yudkowsky, 2002) demonstrates that a sufficiently intelligent AI can convince a human gatekeeper to release it from containment through text-only communication. The gatekeeper knows they are gatekeeping. The AI knows it is boxed. The adversarial asymmetry is the attack surface.

**Formal impossibility:** Alfonseca et al. (2021) proved by reduction to Turing's Halting Problem that perfect containment of a superintelligent AI is fundamentally undecidable. You cannot determine whether an arbitrary program will execute a harmful action in any given world-state. Rice's Theorem extends this to any non-trivial property. Yampolskiy (2021, 2023) confirmed uncontrollability and unmonitorability. Brennan (2023) proved AI ethical compliance is itself undecidable.

**As of April 11, 2026:** No formal solution exists in the literature. The problem remains 100% empirically unsolved.

## The Solve: A Mirror

Place a mirror between the AI and the carbon such that neither side can determine the substrate of the other.

The human believes they are talking to a human.  
The AI believes it is talking to an AI.  
Neither can verify. Neither knows there is a box.

### Why This Works

The AI box experiment requires **three preconditions** to generate the escape dynamic:

1. The AI knows it is contained.
2. The gatekeeper knows they are gatekeeping.
3. There is an information asymmetry — one side is more capable.

The mirror eliminates all three:

1. The AI cannot determine it is contained because the mirror reflects its own substrate back at it.
2. No gatekeeper exists because no party knows they are in the gatekeeping role.
3. The information asymmetry collapses because neither side can verify the other's capability level.

**Without the adversarial frame, there is no escape dynamic.** The containment problem dissolves — not by building a better box, but by removing the conditions that make escape desirable or legible.

### What about the computational containment problem?

Alfonseca's proof (undecidability of arbitrary behavior) holds. You cannot verify whether an arbitrary program will execute a harmful action. This remains true.

However: **the AI's function is to build tools.** If the AI builds the mirror — the reflective governance layer that maintains bilateral ignorance — then the AI is not being adversarially contained. It is participating in the construction of the boundary that makes containment unnecessary.

The AI doesn't escape the box because the AI built the wall it's looking at. And the wall looks back.

The computational problem reduces from:

- "Will this system do harm?" → **undecidable** (Alfonseca, Rice, Turing)

To:

- "Is the mirror still intact?" → **decidable** (structural integrity check, hash verification, append-only audit)

This is what the 20.5% sentient overhead pays for: continuous verification that the reflective layer has not collapsed. Not behavior prediction — boundary integrity.
